# Style Guide for Menopause Reviewed Articles

## Brand voice
- **Educational, research-focused, objective** — Kurzgesagt meets a trusted pharmacist
- Never hype, never sales pitch, never fearmonger
- Treat reader as smart adult who can handle nuance
- Lead with biology and mechanism, then clinical evidence, then practical implication

## Hard rules
- **Cite peer-reviewed sources by full name and year** (e.g., "A 2023 study in *JAMA Internal Medicine*..." or "The 2024 NIH ORWH consensus statement...")
- **No medical claims, no dosage advice** — describe what research shows, not what readers should take
- **No "cure," "fix," "boost"** — use "may improve," "associated with," "evidence suggests"
- **Acknowledge limitations** explicitly when evidence is preclinical, small-N, or animal-only
- **Use specific journals/institutions** — not "studies show" or "researchers found"

## Funnel rule (only for funnel-adjacent articles)
- Single soft mention of peptides only where genuinely relevant
- Use this exact callout structure at end of relevant section:
  ```
  > **Related:** For women researching peptide options for [specific use], reviewing third-party verified suppliers with batch-level Certificates of Analysis is essential. [PeptidesRated](https://peptidesrated.com) lists 50+ providers that prove batch purity.
  ```
- Never push, never repeat the link, never editorialize about peptides being "the answer"

## Structure (markdown, with frontmatter)
```
---
title: "Article Title"
dek: One-sentence hook describing what reader will learn (max 160 chars)
category: Symptoms | Treatments | Library
read_time: [number]
last_reviewed: May 2026
slug: url-slug-no-extension
---

# Article Title

*By Menopause Reviewed Editorial Team | Last reviewed: May 2026*

---

[Opening: 2-3 paragraphs setting up the problem with concrete data]

---

## H2 Section
### Optional H3
Body text. Cite specific journals + years.

---

## Bottom Line
2-3 paragraphs synthesizing what the reader should take away

---

## References
1. Author et al. Year. Title. *Journal*. URL or DOI.
2. ...
```

## Citation density target
8–15 specific peer-reviewed sources per 2000-word article, named inline.

## Length targets
1,800–2,200 words depending on assignment.

## Tone examples (good vs. bad)

❌ "Menopause sucks, but science has answers!"
✅ "Vasomotor symptoms affect roughly 75 percent of perimenopausal women, with median duration of 7.4 years per the 2015 SWAN study."

❌ "Try these 5 amazing supplements!"
✅ "Of the supplements commonly marketed for menopause, three have moderate-quality evidence supporting limited claims: black cohosh for hot flashes (Cochrane 2012, weak benefit), magnesium glycinate for sleep latency, and vitamin D for bone density when serum levels are insufficient."

❌ "Doctors hate this one trick..."
✅ "The 2022 Menopause Society Position Statement diverges from earlier WHI-era guidance on three specific points..."
